# TOGAF 10 Enterprise Architecture Practitioner (Level 2)

The TOGAF Practitioner certification demonstrates the ability to apply the TOGAF Architecture Development Method (ADM) to real-world enterprise architecture scenarios. This is a professional-level certification requiring both Foundation knowledge and the ability to work through complex, scenario-based problems.

## About

The TOGAF Practitioner certification validates that a candidate can apply TOGAF ADM concepts to practical enterprise architecture scenarios. Rather than testing terminology recall, this exam measures:

- Ability to analyze business problems and architecture challenges
- Competency in selecting appropriate TOGAF ADM phases for solutions
- Understanding of how to apply architecture principles in context
- Capacity to evaluate trade-offs and make architecture decisions
- Skill in justifying architectural approaches with TOGAF reasoning

A TOGAF Practitioner is recognized as capable of:

- Leading or contributing to enterprise architecture initiatives
- Making architecture recommendations based on business context
- Guiding organizations through transformation using TOGAF ADM
- Mentoring junior architects in TOGAF methodology
- Architecting solutions for complex organizational challenges

This is a professional-level certification appropriate for architects in significant organizational roles.

## Exam Format: Scenario-Based Questions

The 8 questions each present a detailed business scenario (150-300 words) followed by a specific architecture question with multiple-choice options. Example scenario structure:

**Scenario Context:**
- Organization description (size, industry, maturity)
- Current-state problems (business, technical, organizational)
- Strategic goals and constraints
- Stakeholder requirements and pressures
- Existing architecture or transformation initiatives

**Question Example:**
*"In Phase B, the architects discover that the business operating model is unclear. Which of the following is the MOST appropriate next step?"*

Options:
- A) Skip Phase B, proceed to Phase C
- B) Conduct a business capability mapping exercise
- C) Request additional funding before proceeding
- D) Implement the existing applications as-is

(Answer: B — demonstrates understanding of business architecture depth and iterative ADM)

Each scenario tests multiple ADM concepts, forcing candidates to think critically about architecture sequencing, stakeholder management, and real-world constraints.

## Domain Context — Enterprise Architecture

Enterprise Architecture at the Practitioner level is about **applied transformation**. Practitioners work in:

- **Digital transformation initiatives** – Aligning legacy systems with cloud/modern architectures
- **Organizational change management** – Guiding large-scale IT/business changes
- **Strategic technology planning** – Multi-year roadmaps supporting business strategy
- **Architecture governance** – Establishing frameworks for consistent decision-making
- **Enterprise integration** – Consolidating fragmented systems post-merger or restructuring
- **Cloud adoption** – Planning cloud migration strategies and hybrid architectures
- **Regulatory compliance** – Architecting for governance, risk, and compliance (GRC)

TOGAF Practitioners are typically found in:
- Large enterprises (Fortune 500, government agencies)
- Management consulting firms (architecture services)
- Systems integrators and IT service companies
- Financial services, healthcare, and telecommunications (highly regulated sectors)
- Technology consulting boutiques

## Topics Covered

### ADM Application in Real-World Contexts

#### Phase A: Architecture Vision (Scenario Application)
- Extracting business requirements from complex organizational contexts
- Defining appropriate scope when constraints are vague
- Establishing vision statements that bridge business and IT
- Managing stakeholder expectations when goals conflict
- Recognizing when to iterate vs. move forward

#### Phase B: Business Architecture (Scenario Application)
- Decomposing complex value chains
- Creating business capability maps
- Defining roles and organizational structures
- Understanding interdependencies between business units
- Aligning capability models to strategy

#### Phase C: Information Systems Architecture (Scenario Application)
- Mapping information flows in complex organizations
- Designing data governance models
- Selecting appropriate application architecture patterns
- Integrating legacy and modern applications
- Planning for information security and privacy

#### Phase D: Technology Architecture (Scenario Application)
- Infrastructure architecture decisions in constrained environments
- Technology selection based on business context
- Cloud vs. on-premise trade-off analysis
- Integration and middleware strategy
- Vendor management and licensing implications

#### Phase E: Opportunities & Solutions (Scenario Application)
- Consolidating diverse requirements into solution packages
- Prioritizing when resources are limited
- Creating implementation roadmaps with realistic sequencing
- Managing technical debt trade-offs
- Building business cases for initiatives

#### Phase F: Migration Planning (Scenario Application)
- Realistic planning for large transformations
- Managing risk during complex implementations
- Sequencing work streams and dependencies
- Resource and budget planning for multi-year programs
- Managing organizational resistance to change

#### Phase G: Implementation Governance (Scenario Application)
- Establishing architecture review boards (ARBs)
- Ensuring solution conformance to architecture
- Handling exceptions and deviation requests
- Maintaining architecture consistency across programs
- Balancing governance with project agility

#### Phase H: Architecture Change Management (Scenario Application)
- Post-implementation architecture updates
- Managing ongoing architecture evolution
- Responding to technology changes and business shifts
- Version management and architecture repository maintenance
- Continuous improvement of architecture processes

## Exam Strategy & Tips

### Before the Exam

1. **Thoroughly master ADM phases** – You must know not just *what* each phase does, but *when* and *why* to use it
2. **Study business context** – Many scenario questions test understanding of business drivers, not technical features
3. **Practice scenario reading** – Learn to extract key information quickly (90 min / 8 questions = ~11 min per question)
4. **Review governance frameworks** – ARB authority, exception handling, and stakeholder escalation are tested heavily
5. **Understand trade-offs** – Scenarios often present multiple valid approaches; questions ask which is "MOST appropriate" given constraints
6. **Do timed practice exams** – Scenario exams require time management; practice under pressure
7. **Review your Foundation knowledge** – If weak on ADM phases, review Foundation materials before attempting Practitioner

### During the Exam

- **Read the entire scenario carefully** – Key constraints are often buried in organizational context
- **Identify the phase/decision** – Determine what phase or architecture decision the question targets
- **Look for the key constraint** – Budget, time, stakeholder conflict, technical limitation — the constraint often determines the right answer
- **Eliminate obviously wrong answers** – In scenario-based questions, some options are clearly outside TOGAF methodology
- **Manage time**: ~11 minutes per scenario question (allows 15 min review)
- **Flag uncertain questions** – Mark and return; don't overthink at first pass

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Not reading scenarios completely** – Missing key constraints leads to wrong answers
- **Confusing "best practice" with "TOGAF methodology"** – Questions ask what TOGAF recommends, not what's generically best
- **Over-thinking simple questions** – Some scenarios are straightforward once you identify the phase
- **Forgetting governance considerations** – Modern TOGAF emphasizes governance; many questions test this
- **Not understanding stakeholder dynamics** – Transformation requires managing people, not just technology
- **Using Foundation-level thinking** – Practitioner requires deeper, more nuanced understanding of ADM

### Scenario Question Types

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Phase sequencing** | "Organization skipped Phase B. What risk are they taking?" |
| **Stakeholder management** | "Board and IT director have conflicting visions. Who should decide?" |
| **Governance** | "How should the ARB handle this architecture exception request?" |
| **Principles conflict** | "Two architecture principles conflict in this scenario. How do you proceed?" |
| **Risk mitigation** | "What architecture risk should this organization address first?" |
| **Roadmapping** | "Given constraints, what sequence makes sense for this multi-year program?" |
| **Building block selection** | "For this scenario, should they buy, build, or reuse this capability?" |
