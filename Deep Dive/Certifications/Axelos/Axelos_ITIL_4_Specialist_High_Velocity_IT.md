## About

ITIL 4 Specialist: High Velocity IT (HVIT) is an intermediate-level credential that validates advanced knowledge of applying ITIL practices in fast-moving, digitally-driven environments. Launched in 2020, it bridges traditional ITSM frameworks with modern DevOps, Agile, Lean, and cloud-native practices. The HVIT credential targets IT service managers, DevOps engineers, and SRE leads who operate in environments requiring rapid delivery, continuous improvement, and high organizational resilience. Unlike Foundation (conceptual) or Practitioner (operational), HVIT demonstrates the ability to adapt ITIL principles to high-velocity contexts where traditional change-management cycles may be incompatible with deployment frequencies of multiple times per day. It is the primary stepping stone from ITIL Practitioner toward ITIL Managing Professional specialization in digital transformation and DevOps.

---

## Domain context — ITSM / DevOps

IT Service Management (ITSM) encompasses the delivery, support, and optimization of IT services aligned with business outcomes. DevOps represents the cultural, organizational, and technical convergence of IT operations and software development—prioritizing automation, continuous delivery, and shared responsibility. HVIT sits at the intersection: it modernizes ITSM for DevOps-driven organizations, addressing the tension between IT governance/control and rapid iteration. In enterprise contexts (financial services, healthcare, telco), HVIT validates your ability to apply ITIL rigor in cloud, containerization, and continuous deployment pipelines.

## Topics covered

The ITIL 4 Specialist: High Velocity IT exam blueprint covers five primary domains:

- **High-Velocity Nature of the Digital Enterprise** (weight: ~20%) — Definition and characteristics of high-velocity IT environments, digital transformation drivers, organizational structures suited to rapid change, and the role of ITSM in supporting digital business models. Focus on continuous delivery, automation, and agile organizational design.

- **ITIL Guiding Principles in High-Velocity IT** (weight: ~20%) — Application of the seven ITIL guiding principles (focus on value, start where you are, progress iteratively, think holistically, keep it simple, optimize and automate, collaborate and promote visibility) within DevOps and agile delivery contexts. Emphasis on feedback loops, metrics, and cultural alignment.

- **Digital Product Lifecycle** (weight: ~25%) — End-to-end management of digital products and services: conception, design, development, deployment, operations, and continuous improvement. Integration of product thinking with ITIL service value chain. Coverage of feature-driven delivery, MVP (minimum viable product) concepts, and cross-functional team responsibilities.

- **Resilient and Secure IT Systems** (weight: ~15%) — Building resilience into high-velocity IT systems through redundancy, fault tolerance, chaos engineering, and observability. Security integration in CI/CD pipelines, infrastructure-as-code scanning, and compliance automation. Modern approaches to risk in fast-moving environments.

- **Techniques for High-Velocity IT** (weight: ~20%) — Practical methodologies and tools: Continuous Integration (CI), Continuous Delivery (CD), Continuous Deployment (CD), containerization, infrastructure-as-code, automated testing, observability platforms, and collaboration tools (version control, issue tracking, alerting). Lean and Agile practices applied to IT service operations.

## Real-world application: Scenario walkthrough

**Scenario:** A financial services firm runs a customer-facing mobile app. Previously, releases occurred quarterly with extensive change-management gatekeeping. The new CTO mandates weekly feature deployments via CI/CD. Legacy ITSM processes cause bottlenecks. How do you apply ITIL in this high-velocity context?

**HVIT principles in action:**

1. **Guiding Principle: Start Where You Are** — Audit existing ITSM practices (CAB, change logs, incident escalation). Identify which serve compliance (audit trails, approval chains) and which are pure friction. Retain the former; automate or eliminate the latter.

2. **Digital Product Lifecycle** — Shift from "IT Service Desk" to "Feature Delivery." User stories flow through a product backlog; each feature release is a versioned artifact with rollback capability. The product owner (business) and engineering lead define what ships; Ops ensures infrastructure scales and alerts appropriately.

3. **Guiding Principle: Optimize and Automate** — Implement CI/CD pipelines:
   - Code commit → automated test suite runs → security scanning (SAST) → artifact built and pushed to staging.
   - Staging validates infrastructure, load testing, compliance checks (data handling, audit logging).
   - Production canary deploy: 5% of traffic → monitor metrics for 10 min → full rollout if healthy.
   - Automated rollback triggered by error rate thresholds.

4. **Resilience and Observability** — Observability replaces traditional CMDB. Distributed tracing (OpenTelemetry) and metrics (Prometheus) track latency, error rates, and dependencies. SRE team owns error budgets: if 99.9% uptime is the SLA, the error budget is ~43 min/month. Teams can "spend" this on deployments or experiments.

5. **Incident Management Reimagined** — High-velocity incidents (app crash, DB performance spike) trigger automated alerts. SRE on-call assesses severity and triggers war room. Post-incident, a blameless post-mortem identifies process gaps. The insight feeds back into the product backlog or infrastructure design. No "root cause" scapegoating; focus on system design improvements.

6. **Guiding Principle: Focus on Value** — Each week's release is measured by user impact: feature adoption rate, customer feedback, error budget burn. If a feature doesn't drive value or burns budget faster than predicted, it's disabled via feature flag (not code revert). This keeps release velocity high while maintaining safety.

7. **Organizational Alignment** — Dev, Ops, and Security sit in the same standups. Infrastructure is defined-as-code (Terraform) in the same Git repo as application code; Ops reviews PRs (pull requests) alongside Dev. Security team provides guardrails (approved libraries, encryption defaults) rather than gates.

**Outcome:** Releases go from quarterly to weekly. Time-to-market for bug fixes drops from 8 weeks to 2 hours. Incident recovery time (MTTR) improves from 4 hours to 15 minutes due to observability and automation. ITSM doesn't disappear—it evolves to support rapid, safe delivery.

## Exam preparation strategy

**Timeline:** 6–10 weeks for candidates with ITIL Foundation and 2+ years IT operations/DevOps experience; 10–14 weeks for Foundation-only candidates.

**Study approach:**

1. **Prerequisite review (weeks 1–2):** If your ITIL Foundation is rusty, re-familiarize yourself with the SVS, guiding principles, and 15 practices. HVIT builds on this foundation; gaps here will haunt deeper study.

2. **Understand high-velocity context (weeks 2–3):** Read introductory resources on DevOps (DORA research, "The Phoenix Project," SRE book excerpts). Understand why traditional ITSM change processes (formal CABs, 30-day change windows) don't fit. This context is essential for scenario questions.

3. **Study the five domains (weeks 3–7):**
   - **High-Velocity Digital Enterprise:** Organizational structures (cross-functional pods, two-pizza teams), metrics, automation drivers.
   - **Guiding Principles:** Deep dive into how each principle applies to DevOps. Create a matrix: principle vs. high-velocity challenge (e.g., "Focus on Value" vs. "Feature Flag Rollout").
   - **Product Lifecycle:** Learn MVP thinking, continuous feedback, and how Ops fits into product discovery and delivery.
   - **Resilience & Security:** Study SRE principles (error budgets, chaos engineering), observability stacks (Prometheus, Grafana), and security-as-code.
   - **Techniques:** Hands-on familiarity with CI/CD concepts (Jenkins, GitLab, GitHub Actions), IaC (Terraform), and monitoring.

4. **Hands-on exploration (weeks 6–8):** If possible, set up a toy CI/CD pipeline (free tier: GitHub Actions) and a monitoring stack (Prometheus + Grafana local Docker). Understanding the mechanics reinforces the concepts.

5. **Practice exams (weeks 8–10):** Take full-length mocks. HVIT emphasizes application and scenario judgment over pure recall. Questions often ask: "Which guiding principle does this situation best exemplify?" or "How would you adapt ITSM practice X for this high-velocity team?"

6. **Final review (week 10):** Re-read the five domains, focusing on edge cases and trade-offs. Example: "When should you *not* automate a deployment?" (Answer: when the change is so risky that manual review is justified and won't bottleneck throughput).

**Common pitfalls to avoid:**
- Treating HVIT as "ITSM for DevOps engineers" — it's equally for IT managers running traditional ops in fast-moving orgs.
- Assuming Agile/DevOps means "no process" — HVIT is about *evolving* process, not eliminating it.
- Memorizing DevOps tooling details — the exam tests principles, not "How do you configure Jenkins?" Know the concepts, not tool syntax.
- Ignoring the product lifecycle domain — this is where HVIT differs most from earlier ITIL levels.

**Passing rate:** ~70–75% of test-takers pass on the first attempt. Most candidates pass with 6–8 weeks of focused study combined with applied DevOps experience.
