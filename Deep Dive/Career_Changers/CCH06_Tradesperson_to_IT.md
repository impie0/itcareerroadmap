# Career Changer Guide: Tradesperson → IT

**`CCH06`** · _Tradesperson (Electrician, Plumber, HVAC, Construction)_ **→** _IT Support, Data Centre & OT/ICS Security_ · _6–12 months to first IT job_

> **Who this is for:** You've spent years working with your hands, solving physical problems, diagnosing why systems don't work, and taking pride in reliability and precision. You understand electrical systems, infrastructure, power management, and physical security. This guide shows you how to transition these hands-on, systems-thinking skills into IT—where your practical troubleshooting mindset and infrastructure understanding are highly valued, especially in data centre operations and OT security roles.

---

## Your Starting Position

### What your current career gives you

Tradespersons bring something to IT that university graduates often lack: practical, hands-on problem-solving discipline and comfort with complex infrastructure. You're used to diagnosing problems (Is the power on? Is the circuit correct?), testing solutions methodically, and taking responsibility for systems that must not fail. You understand electrical systems, network infrastructure (if cabling background), and physical security. You know the importance of proper documentation, following procedures, and doing things right the first time. You're comfortable working in physically challenging environments, you understand safety protocols, and you respect the importance of system reliability. These are exactly the mindsets data centre operators and infrastructure technicians need. Additionally, your electrical background is directly applicable to data centre power systems, uninterruptible power supplies (UPS), electrical infrastructure—knowledge most IT-only graduates lack.

### Transferable skills inventory

| Skill from Tradesperson Work | How it maps to IT |
|---|---|
| Hands-on problem diagnosis and troubleshooting | IT troubleshooting methodology, hardware diagnostics, infrastructure problem-solving |
| Electrical systems understanding | Data centre power systems, UPS management, electrical infrastructure, OT security |
| Infrastructure and physical systems knowledge | Data centre operations, network cabling infrastructure, physical infrastructure |
| Safety protocols and compliance mindset | IT security protocols, data centre safety standards, compliance procedures |
| Precision and documentation discipline | Change management, system documentation, infrastructure documentation, maintenance logs |

| Equipment maintenance and preventive care | IT infrastructure maintenance, preventive measures, system reliability focus |

### What you'll need to build from scratch

| Gap area | How to address it |
|---|---|
| Networking fundamentals and IT protocols (TCP/IP, DNS) | CompTIA A+ and Network+ courses, YouTube networking basics |
| Operating systems and software (Windows, Linux) | CompTIA A+, YouTube OS fundamentals, hands-on labs |

### Honest difficulty assessment

**Rating: Easy to Moderate**

This is one of the easier transitions to IT because your hands-on troubleshooting mindset translates directly. You don't need to unlearn anything—you just need to learn new technical content. Your practical problem-solving skills are uncommon in IT and genuinely valued, especially in data centre and infrastructure roles. The main challenge is that IT uses different terminology and different tools, not different thinking. Your learning curve is shorter than most career-changers.

The secondary consideration: your trade background is most valued in infrastructure and data centre roles, less so in pure help desk support. However, help desk is still viable and is your quickest path to first IT job. Many tradespeople move from IT support → infrastructure → data centre → OT security over 2–3 years.

## Why IT Employers Value Tradesperson Backgrounds

Data centre operators (Teraco, Africa Data Centres), infrastructure companies, and OT/ICS specialists actively seek tradesperson backgrounds because infrastructure understanding is rare among pure IT graduates. A 2023 LinkedIn analysis found that data centre technician job postings explicitly mention "electrical background preferred" or "infrastructure experience valued." Companies know that someone who understands power systems, cabling, and physical infrastructure hits the ground running.

Real examples: A former electrician working in a UK data centre was promoted to data centre manager because his electrical understanding enabled him to optimize power efficiency—something IT-only managers couldn't do. In South Africa, Eskom's IT department actively recruits electricians for OT security roles because they understand electrical systems and operational reliability.

Tradespersons transition most successfully into: (1) Data Centre Technician roles (infrastructure operations), (2) IT Support (help desk as entry point, then infrastructure), (3) Network Cabling Technician (if cabling background), (4) OT/ICS Security Specialist (if electrical or HVAC background).

## Recommended Career Target

### Primary target: IT Support Technician / Data Centre Technician

**Why this role fits your background:**

IT Support is your fastest entry point (6–8 months), and it leverages your troubleshooting mindset directly. You'll help users with computer problems, much like you diagnosed problems in electrical or HVAC systems. Your hands-on approach is an asset. Data Centre Technician roles (longer path, 12–18 months) are your optimal long-term role, where your electrical and infrastructure background becomes extremely valuable. You'll manage power systems, cooling, cabling infrastructure, physical security—work that combines your trade skills with IT.

**Salary at entry level:**
- USD: $45,000–$70,000/year
- ZAR: R28,000–R45,000/month
- GBP: £32,000–£50,000/year

**Typical employers for this background:**
- Data centre operators (Teraco, Africa Data Centres, DataPro, MainOne)
- Managed Service Providers (BCX, Nile Online, Bytes Technology Group)
- Corporate IT infrastructure teams
- Critical infrastructure operators (Eskom, Transnet OT security)
- Telecommunications infrastructure (Vodacom, MTN)

## Interview Preparation

### The "why IT?" question for tradesperson backgrounds

Employers may wonder: why leave a stable trade? You need a clear answer.

**Framework for your answer:**
"I've spent [X years] diagnosing and fixing complex infrastructure systems. I realized I'm more interested in the digital infrastructure that runs businesses—servers, networks, data centres. IT infrastructure lets me apply the same troubleshooting and reliability mindset I've developed, but to systems that affect hundreds or thousands of people. Data centre operations especially appeal to me because it's infrastructure work: power systems, cooling, reliability—areas where my background is directly applicable. This is a natural progression for my career, not leaving it."

**Variation for data centre path:**
"Data centre operations combine physical infrastructure with IT systems—power, cooling, physical security, network infrastructure. My background in [electrical/HVAC/infrastructure] is exactly what data centres need alongside IT people."

### Common technical interview questions for IT Support roles

1. **Q: "Walk me through how you'd troubleshoot a computer that won't turn on."**
   → **A:** Systematically: 1) Check power connections (like checking if breaker is on), 2) Check for indicator lights or beeps (POST codes), 3) Test with different power outlet, 4) If fans start but no display: check RAM, monitor connection, 5) If nothing: likely power supply or motherboard. Your troubleshooting methodology from trades applies directly. Study: CompTIA A+ hardware troubleshooting.

2. **Q: "What are the layers of the OSI model and how would you use them for troubleshooting?"**
   → **A:** 7 layers: Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application. Troubleshooting: start at Layer 1 (is it plugged in?), work up. If Layer 1–3 fine, problem is higher layer (software/application). Your layered troubleshooting thinking applies. Study: CompTIA A+ OSI model.

3. **Q: "Describe your experience working with complex systems and managing reliability."**
   → **A:** Use your trade background: "In [trade], I maintained critical systems that couldn't fail. I developed: preventive maintenance practices, systematic troubleshooting, documentation discipline, attention to detail. IT infrastructure is similar—systems must be reliable, documented, properly maintained." Your background is the asset here.

4. **Q: "How would you approach learning new IT systems quickly?"**
   → **A:** "Like learning new equipment in my trade: start with manuals/documentation, understand how components interact, test carefully, ask experienced people questions, practice, document what I learn. I'm comfortable learning from documentation and hands-on experience."

5. **Q: "Tell me about a time you diagnosed a complex problem."**
   → **A:** Use a real example from your trade: "A system was failing intermittently. I: 1) documented when failures occurred, 2) tested each component, 3) looked for patterns, 4) identified root cause, 5) fixed it. Applied same methodology to IT support." Shows: systematic thinking, documentation discipline.

### Red flags to avoid in interviews

- **"I don't know anything about computers."** Wrong. You're CompTIA A+ certified. Say: "I'm new to IT professionally, but I have strong foundational knowledge and I learn technical concepts quickly."

- **"I just need a job change."** Don't lead with "leaving the trade." Lead with: "IT infrastructure appeals to me because I can apply my troubleshooting and reliability expertise in a digital context."

- **"I'm better with hands-on work than computers."** Avoid suggesting IT is less hands-on. Say: "I thrive on hands-on problem-solving and infrastructure work—both of which are central to IT support and data centre operations."

## South Africa Context

### Is this career change viable in SA?

Absolutely yes. SA's critical infrastructure (Eskom, Transnet) and data centre operators (Teraco, Africa Data Centres, MainOne) desperately need people with infrastructure backgrounds. Data centre technician roles are high-demand, and electricians have a natural advantage. MSPs and corporate IT departments also value people with practical infrastructure thinking.

BEE/EE: Trade background (artisan certification) + IT certification is excellent for small business and employee equity targets. Some large companies specifically seek "skilled workers transitioning to IT" for diversity initiatives.

Remote work: Less viable for data centre work (on-site required), but IT support roles increasingly remote. Data centre work pays more and requires on-site presence.

### SA success story

**Thandi's journey (representative story):**
Thandi was a qualified electrician in Johannesburg, earning R18,000/month installing electrical systems. She was skilled but wanted better career prospects and better work-life balance. In 2023, at age 32, she decided to transition to IT infrastructure.

She completed Google IT Support Certificate (3 months, R3,000) and CompTIA A+ (2 months, R2,500 study + R4,100 exam). Total cost: R9,600. She built a portfolio: home network documentation, troubleshooting case studies, Windows administration guide.

She applied to Teraco Data Centres for a Junior Data Centre Technician role. They hired her at R36,000/month—double her electrician salary. Why? Her electrical background was directly applicable: she understood power systems, cooling infrastructure, electrical safety—things most IT-only graduates don't know.

After 18 months at Teraco, earning R48,000/month, she was promoted to Senior Data Centre Technician. By year 3, she's earning R62,000/month with specialization in OT security. Her investment of 5 months and R9,600 has completely transformed her career trajectory.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm 45+ years old. Is age a barrier?**

No, especially with trade background. You bring reliability, discipline, and systems thinking. Age is an asset in infrastructure and data centre roles where experience matters. Avoid mentioning age; focus on skills and certifications.

**Q: My trade pays OK. Why transition to IT?**

Better long-term trajectory. Trade work often caps out at R20,000–R25,000/month. IT infrastructure can reach R55,000–R100,000+/month. Better work-life balance. More career flexibility.

**Q: Do I need a degree or bootcamp?**

No. CompTIA A+ + Google IT Support + portfolio gets you hired for IT support. Bootcamp not necessary. Only consider if you struggle with self-study.

**Q: Can I stay in electrician/trade work and add IT as side?**

You could, but you'd be competing against full-time IT people with better certifications. Full transition gives you better career prospects and higher pay.

**Q: How long before I'm earning significantly more?**

Immediately. Entry IT support (R28,000–R45,000) is 1.5–2.5x typical trade salaries. By year 3–4 in data centre operations, you can be earning 2.5–3x your starting trade salary.

**Q: What's the hardest part of this transition?**

Learning new terminology and working in office/data centre environments rather than field work. Your troubleshooting mindset is the hard part—you already have it. Everything else is learning new content.
