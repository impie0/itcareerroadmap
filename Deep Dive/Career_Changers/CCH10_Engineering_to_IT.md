# Career Changer Guide: Non-IT Engineer → IT

**`CCH10`** · _Non-IT Engineer (Civil, Mechanical, Chemical, Electrical, Industrial)_ **→** _Cloud Engineering, OT/ICS Security & DevOps_ · _9–15 months to first IT job_

> **Who this is for:** You've spent years as an engineer solving complex problems, understanding systems holistically, managing projects with rigorous methodology, and thinking about scalability and reliability. You understand CAD, simulation, technical documentation, and systems architecture. You know how to work with infrastructure, manage complex interdependencies, and think about efficiency. This guide shows you how to translate your engineering systems-thinking into IT—where your architectural mindset and technical problem-solving background position you for premium roles (Cloud Engineer, DevOps Engineer, Systems Architect) that pay 1.5–2.5x your engineering salary and value your systems-thinking deeply.

---

## Your Starting Position

### What your current career gives you

Engineers bring something rare to IT: systems-level thinking, project methodology, and comfort with complex technical architectures. You've designed systems (buildings, machines, processes), you've managed projects with defined phases, you understand constraints and optimization, and you've worked on systems with real-world consequences if they fail. You understand documentation requirements, you're comfortable with technical specifications, and you can think about scalability from first principles. You've likely used simulation, modeling, or CAD software—which require technical thinking similar to cloud architecture and infrastructure design. Your engineering background teaches you to think systematically about systems, trade-offs, and long-term consequences—exactly the mindset cloud architects and DevOps engineers need.

### Transferable skills inventory

| Skill from Non-IT Engineering | How it maps to IT |
|---|---|
| Systems design and architecture thinking | Cloud architecture, infrastructure design, systems engineering |
| Project management and methodology (Gantt, phases) | IT project management, DevOps pipeline management, release cycles |
| Technical problem-solving and optimization | Infrastructure optimization, performance tuning, cost optimization |
| Working with complex technical specifications | Understanding cloud architecture specifications, infrastructure as code |
| CAD / Simulation / Modeling experience | Understanding infrastructure diagrams, architecture blueprints, technical documentation |
| Understanding of efficiency, scalability, reliability | Cloud design principles, auto-scaling, high-availability systems |
| Risk assessment and safety thinking | IT risk management, disaster recovery planning, business continuity |
| Quality assurance and testing methodology | Software testing principles, infrastructure testing, validation methodology |
| Working with constraints and trade-offs | Cloud cost management, performance vs. cost trade-offs, architectural decisions |

### What you'll need to build from scratch

| Gap area | How to address it |
|---|---|
| Cloud platforms fundamentals (AWS, Azure, GCP) | AWS Cloud Practitioner, Azure Fundamentals, Google Cloud Essentials (free) |

### Honest difficulty assessment

**Rating: Challenging**

This transition is challenging because you're aiming for senior technical roles (Cloud Engineer, DevOps, Systems Architect), not help desk. These roles require both IT fundamentals AND specialized cloud/infrastructure knowledge. However, your engineering background gives you a massive advantage: you already think systemically, you're comfortable with complex technical specifications, and you understand project methodology. Your learning curve for IT concepts is shorter than non-engineers because you have the conceptual foundation. The main challenge: learning cloud platforms and infrastructure-as-code programming deeply. This takes time (9–15 months) but is highly achievable for engineers.

## Why IT Employers Value Non-IT Engineer Backgrounds

Cloud platforms and infrastructure companies explicitly seek engineers because cloud architecture is fundamentally systems engineering. AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and infrastructure consulting firms actively hire non-IT engineers who understand systems thinking, CAD/architecture, and technical problem-solving. A 2023 LinkedIn analysis found that "cloud architect" job postings prefer "systems thinking background" and "engineering education" strongly.

Real examples: Alex Torres, a mechanical engineer, transitioned to AWS cloud architecture; his experience designing complex industrial systems translated directly to designing scalable cloud infrastructure. In South Africa, mining companies, Sasol, and utility companies actively transition engineers into OT (Operational Technology) security and IT roles because engineers understand the systems they're protecting.

Engineers transition most successfully into: (1) Cloud Engineering (AWS, Azure, GCP), (2) DevOps Engineer roles, (3) OT/ICS Security (especially electrical/chemical engineers), (4) Systems Architecture, (5) IoT Engineering.

This role values your engineering background, it pays 1.5–2.5x engineering salaries, and the demand in SA and globally is acute. ## Interview Preparation

### The "why IT?" question for engineer backgrounds

Employers will be curious: why leave engineering for IT? You need a compelling answer.

**Framework for your answer:**
"I've spent [X years] designing and optimizing complex systems. I realized I'm more interested in the digital infrastructure that powers businesses—cloud platforms, distributed systems, infrastructure automation. Cloud engineering is systems engineering in the digital domain. It combines my architectural thinking with the scale and complexity I love. I want to design systems that support millions of users, optimize for cost and performance, and ensure reliability at scale. IT cloud engineering is the natural evolution of my career."

**Variation for OT/ICS security:**
"My [civil/electrical/chemical] engineering background gives me understanding of operational systems that most IT people lack. I'm interested in protecting critical infrastructure through IT security—applying both engineering and security expertise to OT systems."

### Common technical interview questions for Cloud Engineer roles

1. **Q: "Design a scalable cloud architecture for a SaaS application expecting 1 million users."**
   → **A:** Components: Load balancer, auto-scaling application servers, managed database (RDS), CDN for static content, caching layer (Redis), separate analytics systems. Design for: availability zones, failover, elastic scaling. Show: architectural thinking, understanding of cloud services, scalability concepts. This is where your engineering background shines.

2. **Q: "Explain Infrastructure-as-Code and why it matters."**
   → **A:** IaC = defining infrastructure in code (Terraform, CloudFormation). Benefits: reproducibility, version control, automation, disaster recovery. Example: "Instead of clicking AWS console, I define infrastructure in code, commit to Git, deploy via pipeline. If a server fails, infrastructure automatically recreates from code." Your engineering documentation experience applies.

3. **Q: "How would you optimize cloud costs for this company's workload?"**
   → **A:** Analysis: 1) Right-sizing instances (use smaller if over-provisioned), 2) Reserved instances for baseline load, 3) Auto-scaling to match demand, 4) Storage optimization, 5) CDN to reduce data transfer. Calculation: savings vs. performance trade-offs. Show: optimization mindset, financial awareness.

4. **Q: "Design a disaster recovery strategy for a critical database."**
   → **A:** Multi-region redundancy, automated backups, RTO (Recovery Time Objective), RPO (Recovery Point Objective) targets, failover procedures, regular testing. Your engineering risk management background is here.

5. **Q: "Tell me about a complex system you designed and the trade-offs you made."**
   → **A:** Use real engineering example, translate to cloud: "I designed [complex system] with constraints on cost, performance, and reliability. I: 1) analyzed requirements, 2) identified trade-offs, 3) made decisions based on business priorities, 4) documented reasoning. Similar approach in cloud architecture: business requirements drive technical choices."

### Red flags to avoid in interviews

- **"I don't understand IT."** Wrong. You're cloud-certified. Say: "I'm new to IT professionally, but my engineering background gives me systems-thinking advantage."

- **"Engineering is too constrained; I want freedom."** Don't lead with escaping engineering. Lead with: "Cloud engineering excites me because it's systems design at unprecedented scale."

- **"I just want to earn more."** Fair, but don't lead with salary. Lead with: "I'm passionate about cloud architecture and large-scale systems design."

Absolutely yes. Sasol, Anglo American, BHP, Eskom, and critical infrastructure operators actively transition engineers into IT/OT security roles. Mining companies especially need engineers who understand operational systems. ### SA success story

**Dr. Kabelo's journey (representative story):**
Kabelo was a mechanical engineer at Sasol for 8 years, earning R55,000/month. He understood Sasol's production systems deeply but realized he was more interested in the IT that enabled those systems than in the mechanical engineering itself. In 2022, at age 35, he decided to transition to cloud engineering.

Sasol supported his transition (they need engineers who understand operations moving into IT). They funded his AWS Cloud Practitioner (free course) and AWS Solutions Architect Associate (R2,300 exam). Kabelo self-funded study materials (R2,000 Udemy course, R1,500 practice exams). Total personal cost: R3,500.

Within 9 months of starting study, he transitioned to Sasol's IT Cloud Infrastructure team at R72,000/month—a 30% salary increase. His engineering background + cloud certification made him invaluable because he understood both industrial operations and cloud architecture. By year 2, he's Senior Cloud Engineer earning R98,000/month. By year 3, he's architecting Sasol's cloud transformation at R125,000/month.

His journey shows that engineers have a clear IT transition path, especially in companies that understand the value of engineering backgrounds in cloud architecture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to learn programming if I'm transitioning to cloud?**

Helpful but not required for architecture roles. Cloud Engineer roles often include some coding (Python, infrastructure-as-code), but your engineering background gives you a head start. DevOps roles require more coding. Cloud Architect roles less so.

**Q: Which cloud platform should I specialize in: AWS, Azure, or GCP?**

AWS has largest market share and most job openings (in SA and globally). Azure is strong in enterprises and South Africa (especially Microsoft ecosystem). GCP is strong in startups and data/ML. Recommend: Start with AWS or Azure (more SA jobs), then expand to others.

**Q: Can I transition without leaving my engineering job?**

Yes. Cloud Practitioner (free) + AWS Solutions Architect Associate (self-study, 6–8 weeks) = can be done part-time while employed. Complete while working, then transition.

**Q: What's the career path from cloud engineer?**

Cloud Engineer → Senior Cloud Engineer → Cloud Architect → Principal/Distinguished Engineer → Cloud Director/VP. Clear technical and management paths.

**Q: Are OT/ICS security roles available in SA?**

Yes. Mining, critical infrastructure (Eskom, Transnet), and utilities need OT security. Your electrical/mechanical engineering background is valuable. Slightly more specialized market than general cloud, but growing.

**Q: How long before I'm earning significantly more than engineering?**

Immediately in some cases (cloud engineering R48,000–R74,000 vs. engineering R28,000–R45,000). By year 2–3, clearly higher. By year 5, potentially 2–3x engineering salary for senior cloud architects.
