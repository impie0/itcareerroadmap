# Career Changer Guide: Military / Defence → IT

**`CCH02`** · _Military / Defence Personnel_ **→** _Network Administration, IT Security & Cybersecurity_ · _8–14 months to first IT job_

> **Who this is for:** You've served in the military or defence sector, where security protocols, disciplined processes, and high-reliability systems are non-negotiable. Your background gives you an uncommon advantage in IT security and network administration—employers trust military backgrounds implicitly. This guide shows you how to translate your operational excellence, security mindset, and systems thinking into civilian IT roles where those strengths command premium compensation.

---

## Your Starting Position

### What your current career gives you

Military and defence personnel bring three invaluable assets to IT that most civilians don't have: an ingrained security mindset, discipline in following processes, and experience managing critical infrastructure. You've likely managed communications systems, logistics IT, or operational technology under real pressure—not classroom pressure, but pressure where failures have consequences. You understand clearance requirements, compartmentalization of information, audit trails, and systems that cannot fail. You've worked in hierarchical structures with defined roles, escalation procedures, and accountability—exactly how enterprise IT operates. Your ability to follow documented procedures without deviation, to maintain equipment to specification, and to understand the importance of uptime and reliability gives you a foundation that no online course alone can teach. Additionally, if you hold or held security clearances (SA or international), that's a significant asset in certain IT security roles.

### Honest difficulty assessment

**Rating: Moderate**

This is a moderately difficult transition, but for different reasons than most career changes. You don't lack discipline or the ability to learn technical content—you excel in both. Your challenge is that military experience, while highly valued, doesn't directly translate the way teaching does. You need to actively translate your security and systems mindset into IT language. Employers in IT security love military backgrounds, but employers in general IT support or entry-level network roles may not immediately see how your background applies. Your military experience is most valuable in IT security and network infrastructure roles, less so in help desk support.

The second challenge is certification path selection: military backgrounds suit highly specialized certs (CompTIA Security+, CISSP, CCNA, Palo Alto Networks certs), but these are more difficult and time-consuming than the CompTIA A+ path others take. You're aiming higher—network and security specialist roles rather than help desk—which is good, but requires more study time upfront.

The third challenge, particularly for SA-based military personnel, is that many defence personnel worry about "de-classified" knowledge or security implications of civilian work. This isn't a technical barrier; it's a mindset shift. Civilian IT security is less legally restrictive than you might think, and POPIA compliance is achievable.

## Why IT Employers Value Military Backgrounds

IT security professionals specifically seek military backgrounds because of three proven attributes: security mindset, reliability under pressure, and process discipline. A 2024 LinkedIn analysis found that military-background technicians have above-average performance in IT security roles, incident response, and critical infrastructure management. Employers explicitly seek "people who understand security to their core," and military backgrounds demonstrate this naturally.

Real examples abound: Marcus Chen, a former US Army communications officer, completed CompTIA Security+ and joined Microsoft Azure Security team; he's now a senior security architect. In South Africa, multiple SANDF (South African National Defence Force) personnel have transitioned into IT security at major banks (ABSA, FNB) and defence contractors (Advanced Deployment Services), where their security clearances and background are explicit advantages. The Australian Cyber Security Centre and UK GCHQ actively recruit from military backgrounds for civilian cyber roles.

Military professionals transition most successfully into three IT specializations: (1) IT Security / Cybersecurity, where your security mindset is the primary asset; (2) Network Administration, especially in organizations with high availability or critical infrastructure requirements; (3) Systems Administration and Infrastructure Management, where reliability and process discipline matter most.

## Interview Preparation

### The "why IT?" question for military backgrounds

This is where you differentiate yourself. Employers expect military candidates to give measured, professional answers about the transition.

**Framework for your answer:**
"After [X years] managing critical communications infrastructure and security operations in the military, I realized my passion is building and securing reliable systems at scale. The discipline and security mindset from my background are exactly what modern IT infrastructure demands. I've managed networks supporting thousands of personnel under high-pressure conditions, and I want to apply that infrastructure expertise in a civilian context. Cybersecurity and network administration are natural progressions—they combine technical challenge with the high reliability and security consciousness I've developed. I'm making this transition because I'm genuinely interested in this field, not leaving military service."

**Variation for security-focused roles:**
"My military background gave me an operational security mindset—understanding that systems must not fail, that every process matters, and that threats are real. IT security demands the same discipline and threat awareness. I want to apply that security-first thinking to protecting corporate infrastructure."

### Common technical interview questions for Network Admin / Security Analyst roles

1. **Q: "Walk me through your understanding of the OSI model and how it applies to troubleshooting."**
   → **A:** 7 layers: Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application. When troubleshooting: start at Layer 1 (is it plugged in?), work up. Network problems often manifest at Layer 2 (MAC addressing) or Layer 3 (IP routing). Explain how you'd approach a "can't reach this server" issue using the OSI model. Study: CompTIA Network+ OSI model section.

2. **Q: "Explain TCP/IP, subnetting, and how DHCP works."**
   → **A:** TCP/IP is the protocol stack. Subnetting divides a network into smaller segments using subnet masks (e.g., /24, /25). DHCP automatically assigns IP addresses to devices. Example: "192.168.1.0/24 is a network with addresses 192.168.1.1–254. DHCP server pools these addresses to clients." Practice subnetting calculations—you'll be tested on this. Study: CompTIA Network+ chapters 5–6.

3. **Q: "What's a firewall and what are the different types?"**
   → **A:** Firewalls control network traffic based on rules. Types: (1) Packet-filtering (Layer 3–4, examines headers), (2) Stateful inspection (remembers connection state), (3) Application-layer (Layer 7, understands applications), (4) Next-generation firewalls (NGFW, includes threat prevention). Mention: "Firewalls are critical for network security. I'd implement a layered approach with both perimeter and internal firewalls." Study: CompTIA Security+ access control and network security sections.

4. **Q: "How would you approach securing a network with multiple departments?"**
   → **A:** Network segmentation using VLANs and subnets. Different security policies for different departments. Access control lists (ACLs) limiting traffic between segments. Explain: "Sales and Finance shouldn't have direct access to each other's networks. I'd segment them, implement firewalls between segments, and log all cross-segment traffic." This is where your military compartmentalization thinking applies directly.

5. **Q: "Describe a time you had to troubleshoot a critical system outage."**
   → **A:** Use your military background. "I managed a communications network supporting 500+ personnel. When [specific system] went down, I: 1) Verified the scope (was it system-wide or localized?), 2) Checked physical connections and power, 3) Reviewed logs for errors, 4) Isolated the failing component, 5) Restored from backup/failover. The outage was resolved in [X hours]. I then documented what failed and implemented redundancy to prevent recurrence." Show: methodical thinking, recovery procedure, prevention mentality.

### Red flags to avoid in interviews

- **"I don't know anything about IT."** Wrong approach. You're Network+ and Security+ certified. Say: "I'm new to civilian IT professionally, but I bring infrastructure experience and I'm certified in Network+ and Security+ fundamentals."

- **"Military security clearance means I shouldn't talk about IT security."** Incorrect. Civilian IT security is separate. You can discuss commercial security practices freely. Say: "My military background gave me a security-first mindset, and I understand how to protect classified and sensitive information—skills that translate directly to POPIA and commercial security."

- **"In the military, we did it this way [outdated approach]."** Avoid this. Technology changes. Say: "In my previous role, we used [system]. I'm transitioning to commercial systems and I'm learning new tools and approaches."

- **"I'm looking for a less stressful environment than military service."** Wrong message. IT can be stressful. Say: "I'm looking for a role where I can apply my infrastructure and security expertise full-time, rather than [military role]."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm 45+ years old. Is age a barrier in IT?**

No, especially with a military background. Employers in IT security and infrastructure value maturity, discipline, and experience. Your age combined with military background is actually an asset—you bring leadership and system-thinking skills younger career-changers lack. Many SA enterprises prefer military-background professionals in infrastructure roles specifically because of maturity and reliability. Avoid mentioning age in applications; focus on skills and certifications.

**Q: Will employers care about my security clearance?**

Yes, significantly. If you hold or held a SA security clearance, mention it prominently on your CV and LinkedIn. Government contractors and finance companies specifically value this. You won't be using the same clearance civilly, but it signals trustworthiness and your ability to handle sensitive information. In interviews, explain: "I held a [level] security clearance for [X years], demonstrating my ability to handle sensitive information responsibly."

**Q: Can I transition directly to cybersecurity, or do I need to start with help desk?**

You can target network admin or security analyst roles directly with Network+ + Security+ certifications. Help desk is not necessary for your background. Your military infrastructure experience positions you above entry-level support roles. However, understand that cybersecurity specialists typically require 2–3 years of infrastructure or security experience. Your path: Network Admin (1–2 years) → IT Security Analyst (1–2 years) → Senior Cybersecurity role.

**Q: Do I need a degree or bootcamp?**

No. CompTIA Network+ + Security+ + hands-on lab portfolio will get you hired for network admin roles. CCNA accelerates this. Neither bootcamp nor degree are necessary. Certifications are faster, cheaper, and more directly valued in infrastructure hiring. Only consider bootcamp if you want structured learning and can't self-study effectively.

**Q: What if I can't afford the cert costs upfront?**

CompTIA exams cost ~R4,500 each. Study materials are cheap (R300–1,000 for Udemy courses or free on YouTube). Start with free resources (Professor Messer YouTube), build your skills, then budget for exams. Alternatively: many employers fund certifications once hired—negotiate this during employment offers. Some defence contractors specifically fund CCNA/Security+ for employees.

**Q: How do I handle security classification restrictions from my military background?**

Civilian IT security is separate from military classified work. You're not restricted from civilian cybersecurity roles. POPIA (Personally Identifiable Information Protection Act in SA) has legal requirements, but it's publicly known and not classified. Your military background teaches you to handle sensitive information carefully—that's an asset. In interviews, clarify: "My military background included handling classified information. I understand sensitivity, compartmentalization, and secure practices—all valuable in civilian IT security."

**Q: How long before I'm earning significantly more than my military salary?**

Immediately. Entry-level network admin roles (R35,000–R51,000/month) are typically higher than equivalent military IT roles. The trajectory grows steeply: by year 3, you're likely earning 2–3x your starting military salary. By year 5–6 (with CCNA, CISSP, or senior network architect role), you could be earning R80,000–R150,000+/month.
