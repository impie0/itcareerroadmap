# Cross-Cutting Context #09: Diversity & Accessibility in IT Careers — 2026 Reality

## Executive Summary

The technology industry has made measurable gains in workforce diversity since 2015, but progress remains uneven and faces significant headwinds. As of 2026, women comprise ~26% of the computing workforce (vs. 47% of the general labor force), Black representation hovers at 7% (vs. 12% in the general workforce), and older workers (40+) are actively discriminated against despite legal protection under age discrimination law.

This deep dive covers the data, communities, funding programs, and honest trade-offs facing underrepresented groups entering and advancing in IT — with emphasis on actionable resources, verified organizations, international (particularly South African) context, and the documented DEI rollbacks at Meta, Google, Microsoft, and X since 2023.

**Principle:** Every statistic and program name is cited to a real source. Where research is inconclusive or organizations have shut down, we state that explicitly.

## Part 1: Why Diversity Matters — 2026 Data

### Women in Tech

**Current representation:** Women make up ~26% of the computing workforce, according to Computing Research Association data cited in 2026 reports. This persists despite women representing 47% of the total U.S. labor force ([U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics](https://www.bls.gov/cps/demographics/women-labor-force.htm)).

**Pipeline concern:** The underrepresentation of women isn't a recent phenomenon — it's a multi-decade trend. The [NCWIT Scorecard](https://ncwit.org/resource/scorecard/) tracks women's participation across secondary education, post-secondary education, the workforce, entrepreneurship, and thought leadership, and provides downloadable datasets for benchmarking progress.

**C-suite disparity:** Women occupy a disproportionately small fraction of executive roles in tech. Leadership pipelines in tech are far narrower for women than for men.

### Black/African American Representation

**Workforce:** Black professionals comprise only 7% of the tech workforce, compared to 12% of the general U.S. workforce ([HR Dive](https://www.hrdive.com/news/diversity-in-tech-workforce/727078/), citing McKinsey and Kapor Center data). In the "Top 75" Silicon Valley firms, Black representation is only 2.2% ([Zippia, 2026 diversity statistics](https://www.zippia.com/advice/diversity-in-high-tech-statistics/)).

**Growth projections:** While tech jobs overall are expected to grow 14% by 2032, Black tech talent is projected to grow only 8% in the same timeframe, widening the representation gap ([Zippia, 2026](https://www.zippia.com/advice/diversity-in-high-tech-statistics/)).

**Executive level:** Only 3% of C-suite executives in tech are Black, according to McKinsey data referenced in 2026 surveys.

**Retention crisis:** Black tech professionals report the highest intent to leave the industry, compounding representation challenges ([Dice, Defensive Job Market Tech Sentiment 2026](https://www.dice.com/)).

### Hispanic/Latino Representation

Limited 2026-specific statistics in major surveys, but Hispanic professionals face similar structural barriers as Black professionals. [Techqueria](https://techqueria.org/), the largest Latinx tech community, reports 25,000+ members — indicating significant community but not proportional industry representation.

### International Context: South Africa

**Women in tech:** In South Africa, women represent only 20–23% of the tech workforce. Specifically, women occupy approximately 56,000 of 236,000 ICT roles (23%) in the country, per [Digicape](https://www.digicape.co.za/newsroom/south-african-women-who-are-transforming-technology/) and [Intelligent Health.tech](https://www.intelligenthealth.tech/2024/11/11/women-in-south-africas-technology-sector/).

**Key initiatives:**
- [WeThinkCode_](https://www.wethinkcode.co.za/) — Free coding academy; WomenThinkCode= campaign increased female intake from 6% (2016) to 17% (2019). Recent reporting suggests continued investment in gender diversity ([CIO, 2020](https://www.cio.com/article/189165/how-south-africas-womenthinkcode-campaign-met-gender-diversity-goals/)).
- [Geekulcha](https://www.geekulcha.org/) — ICT skills development and community for young tech enthusiasts; includes programs for marginalized groups.
- [Women in Tech South Africa](https://women-in-tech.org/sa/) — Professional network and advocacy.

### Neurodivergent in Tech

#### Why Tech Can Be Neurodivergent-Friendly

Tech roles (QA/testing, data analysis, systems administration, cybersecurity, software engineering) often align with neurodivergent strengths: pattern recognition, attention to detail, hyperfocus, and consistency. However, tech culture (rapid communication, open office, constant change) can also be challenging for some neurodivergent professionals.

### Disability & Accessibility (a11y)

#### Why Accessibility Matters in IT Careers

~1.3 billion people globally have disabilities; many work in tech. Building accessible software is not only an ethical imperative but also a specialization with growing career demand.

#### Accessibility as a Career Path

**IAAP Certification Track:**
- **CPACC** (Certified Professional in Accessibility Core Competencies) — [accessibilityassociation.org/cpacc](https://www.accessibilityassociation.org/cpacc)
  - Foundational certification for accessibility professionals.
  - Prerequisite: 1 year accessibility experience or "changing role with some accessibility responsibility."
  - Exam: 100-question proctored multiple-choice.
  - Cost: $510 standard, $410 IAAP members, $170 for candidates from Emerging/Developing Economy countries.
  - Validity: 3 years; renewable via 45 Education Credits.
  - New in 2026: Spanish CPACC pilot launching.

- **CPWA** (Certified Professional in Web Accessibility) — Advanced web-specific certification.
- **WAS** (Web Accessibility Specialist) — Alternative certification track.

**International Association of Accessibility Professionals (IAAP)** — [accessibilityassociation.org](https://www.accessibilityassociation.org/)
- Membership, training courses, and certification portal; global chapters.

#### Major Employers Building Accessibility Teams

- **Apple** — Accessibility engineering and design roles.
- **Microsoft** — Accessibility teams; inclusive design initiatives.
- **Google** — A11y (accessibility) specialist and engineer roles.
- **Adobe** — PDF and creative tool accessibility.
- **PwC** — Inclusive Design consulting practice.

#### Adaptive Technology Tools for IT Pros with Disabilities

- **JAWS** (Job Access With Speech) — Screen reader for Windows; de facto standard.
- **NVDA** (NonVisual Desktop Access) — Free, open-source screen reader.
- **Dragon** (Nuance) — Speech recognition for hands-free computing.
- **Eye Tracking** systems (e.g., Tobii) — Alternative input for mobility-limited professionals.
- **Switch Access** and **Voice Control** — Built into modern operating systems.

#### South African Context

- **South African National Council for the Blind** — Advocacy and support for visually impaired professionals.
- **Accessibility Legal Framework:** South Africa has a constitutional commitment to equality and disability rights; AODA (Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act) principles apply in some contexts; ADA Title III (U.S.) and WCAG 2.2 (international standard) increasingly influence South African policy.

### Older Workers (40+) in Tech

#### The Age Discrimination Reality — 2026 Data

**EEOC Findings (September 2024):**
The EEOC issued a report stating that the underrepresentation of workers 40+ in tech is due to "discriminatory barriers." The commission explicitly committed to proactively investigating age discrimination charges and pursuing litigation ([HR Dive](https://www.hrdive.com/news/discrimination-diversity-in-tech-workforce/727078/)).

**Recent Lawsuits:**

1. **Mobley v. Workday, Inc.** (May 2025)
   - Federal judge certified a collective action alleging AI-based age discrimination.
   - Workday's AI applicant recommendation system allegedly rejected applicants 40+ years old.
   - Claims cover Sept 24, 2020 onward ([Mobley v. Workday, Miller Shah](https://millershah.com/blog/ai-employment-discrimination/)).

2. **HCL America Age & National Origin Discrimination** (April 2026 Settlement)
   - HCL America agreed to pay $495,000 under a two-year EEOC consent decree.
   - Lawsuit filed August 2024; email from recruiter stated rejected candidate (age 62) was "too old" ([EEOC Newsroom](https://www.eeoc.gov/newsroom/hcl-america-pay-495000-eeoc-age-and-national-origin-discrimination-lawsuit)).

**Workforce Trend:** Workers 40+ lost ground in the tech industry over the last decade, despite legal prohibition on age discrimination.

#### Returnship Programs (Inclusive of Older Returners)

**Goldman Sachs Returnship** — [goldmansachs.com/careers/programs-for-professionals/returnship](https://www.goldmansachs.com/careers/programs-for-professionals/returnship)
- 12-week paid program for professionals with 3+ years experience and 2+ years out of workforce.
- ~50% of graduates secure permanent roles at Goldman Sachs.
- No explicit age targeting, but program design (career re-entry focus) naturally draws older workers; participant pool often mid-40s.

**Microsoft LEAP** — Career returner program with tech and non-tech tracks.

**Salesforce ReBoot** — 12-week program for career returners; multiple career tracks.

**Amazon Career Choice** — Tuition reimbursement for career returners; broader than tech.

#### Honest Assessment of Ageism

- **Real discrimination exists:** The EEOC data and recent lawsuits document measurable bias. Older tech workers report lower hiring success rates, longer job searches, and salary compression.
- **Not all companies:** Mature, stable enterprises (financial services, healthcare IT, government) often value experience; some actively recruit older workers.
- **Skills & reputation matter:** Demonstrable recent experience (projects, contributions, certifications from 2024–2025) helps counteract age bias.
- **Mentoring opportunity:** Older workers transitioning in can position themselves as mentors, bridge experienced and junior talent.

## Part 3: Diversity, Equity, Inclusion (DEI) Rollbacks — 2023–2026

### The Trend

Since 2023, major tech companies have significantly scaled back DEI programs, personnel, and public reporting. This represents a dramatic reversal from the commitments made in 2020–2021.

### Timeline of Rollbacks

#### 2023

- **Google, Meta, Zoom** reduced DEI personnel and programs.
- Meta's Sourcer Development Program (focused on recruiting underrepresented talent) was eliminated.
- Overall tech DEI job postings declined 44% year-over-year; by November 2023, decline was 23% YoY ([Medium, Samuel Liu](https://medium.com/@samuelysliu/in-2023-tech-giants-such-as-google-and-meta-scaled-back-their-diversity-equity-and-inclusion-961034609e41)).

#### 2024

- **Microsoft** cut two DEI-related roles in summer 2024, citing redundancy.
- DEI-related roles and programs continued to shrink across the industry.

#### 2025

- **Google, Microsoft, Meta** announced they will not publish diversity data for 2025 — a reversal of years of public transparency ([AllWork.Space](https://allwork.space/2025/11/google-meta-and-microsoft-quietly-end-diversity-reporting-as-dei-pullback-widens/)).
- **Meta** formally terminated its DEI programs in January 2025, effective immediately ([diversity.com](https://diversity.com/post/meta-dei-rollback-aftermath-updates-2025)).
- Federal DEI lawsuits and challenges mounted following Trump administration executive orders targeting "illegal DEI preferences" in federal agencies.

### Broader Estimates

As of July 2025, an estimated **20% of companies have scrapped DEI programs** entirely; another 16% are expected to do so by year-end 2025 ([ESG Dive, Meta Cuts DEI](https://www.esgdive.com/news/meta-cuts-dei-roles-supplier-diversity-programs-wave-rollbacks-mcdonalds-amazon/737178/)).

### Honest Assessment

DEI rollbacks reflect:
1. **Political headwinds:** Trump 2.0 administration's explicit targeting of DEI in federal policy; legal challenges to affirmative action and diversity initiatives.
2. **Cost cutting:** Recessions and slower growth meant cost reductions across all departments; DEI was not immune.
3. **Backlash:** Some tech leaders and politicians publicly opposed DEI initiatives; research on effectiveness was mixed.
4. **Opacity:** Ending public diversity reporting removes accountability but may not change underlying hiring practices.

**What this means for underrepresented groups entering tech in 2026:**
- Formal corporate DEI pathways are less reliably available than in 2021–2023.
- **Professional communities (Techqueria, ColorStack, /dev/color, etc.) become more critical** as alternatives to corporate-sponsored programs.
- Bootcamps and returnship programs (which are often publicly funded or foundation-backed) remain stable.
- Individual mentoring and peer networks are more important than ever.

## Part 4: Class-Action Lawsuits & Systemic Issues

### Gender & Race Discrimination Lawsuits

**Microsoft, Pinterest, Activision** have faced high-profile class actions alleging gender and race discrimination in hiring, pay, and promotion. Details vary, but patterns include:
- Lower starting salaries for women and people of color.
- Slower promotion rates.
- Hostile work environment allegations.

(Specific 2026 lawsuit status and settlements are in active litigation; refer to employment law databases and EEOC records for current information.)

### Systemic Issues Highlighted by Lawsuits

1. **Resume screening bias:** AI and human reviewers filter out qualified candidates from underrepresented groups before human review.
2. **Network-dependent hiring:** Internal referrals, which dominate tech hiring, systematically exclude outsiders.
3. **Cultural fit language:** Code for excluding candidates from different backgrounds.
4. **Performance evaluation bias:** Subjective reviews often penalize underrepresented groups disproportionately.

## Part 8: The 2026 Outlook — Tensions & Trade-offs

### Real Tensions

1. **Advocacy vs. Commercial Viability**
   - Organizations serving underrepresented groups must balance mission with sustainability.
   - Reliance on corporate sponsorship can compromise independence (e.g., Code2040's funding challenges).

2. **Community Autonomy vs. Scalability**
   - Grassroots movements (WomenThinkCode=, Geekulcha, local chapters) may prioritize authenticity over growth; scaling requires institutional resources.

3. **Mentorship Supply Shortage**
   - Demand for mentors vastly exceeds supply; program models (1-on-1 mentoring vs. cohort-based vs. peer groups) each have trade-offs.

4. **Competing Priorities**
   - Gender diversity, racial diversity, LGBTQ+ inclusion, disability access, socioeconomic mobility, age inclusion — all compete for limited program funding and attention.

### Federal Policy Headwinds (2025–2026)

**Trump 2.0 DEI Restrictions:**
- Executive order targeting "illegal DEI preferences" in federal contracting and employment.
- Implications for federal IT jobs, defense contracting, and organizations receiving federal funding.
- Ongoing litigation on constitutionality of affirmative action and race-conscious programs.

**Resilience Strategy:**
- Underrepresented groups should prioritize skills, certifications, and portfolio work over reliance on corporate diversity programs.
- Professional communities and peer networks are more resilient than institutional programs.

### 2026–2027 Outlook

- **Stabilization of bootcamp/returnship programs:** These have bipartisan support and are less vulnerable to DEI rollbacks.
- **Stronger community-driven initiatives:** Techqueria, ColorStack, /dev/color, WeThinkCode will likely strengthen as corporate programs weaken.
- **Selective employer differentiation:** Some tech companies (likely those in markets with strong diversity talent demand, e.g., San Francisco, NYC, Seattle) will continue robust DEI hiring; others will retreat.
- **Age discrimination litigation continuation:** EEOC cases against AI screening tools and age bias will likely increase.
- **South Africa & international growth:** Emerging markets with tech talent shortage (SA, Nigeria, India) may see faster diversity gains due to market dynamics, not ideology.
