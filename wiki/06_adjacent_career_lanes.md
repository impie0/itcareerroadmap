# Tech Career Lanes That Don't Fit the Standard Ladder

The traditional tech career path—junior engineer, senior engineer, staff engineer, principal engineer—works for many. But it's far from the only game in town. This guide maps nine alternative career lanes that leverage technical skills while offering different trajectories, compensation models, and lifestyle fits. These aren't "less prestigious" paths; they're genuinely different bets on how to apply expertise.

---

## 1. Sales Engineer / Pre-Sales Solutions Architect

### The Role
A Sales Engineer (SE) is a technical expert embedded in the sales process, translating customer problems into product value. Unlike traditional solutions architects who work post-sale on implementations, SEs focus on winning deals. They demo, write RFPs, explain architecture, address objections, and build proof-of-concept (PoC) systems to help the AE close.

This is not an internal role. SEs work directly with prospects and customers throughout the buying cycle.

### Career Arc
SEs come from two directions:

1. **From engineering**: Senior IC engineer → Sales Engineer → Senior SE → Sales Manager / VP Sales Engineering → VP of Revenue Operations
2. **From sales**: Sales Development Rep (SDR) → Account Executive (AE) → Sales Engineer (sometimes; rarer) → Sales Manager

The engineer-to-SE path is more common. Both converge at the SE level: understanding product *and* psychology.

### Compensation Model
The pay structure is fundamentally different from IC engineering:

- **Base salary**: $130,000–$180,000
- **Commission/variable**: 30–50% of total compensation (OTE = On-Target Earnings)
- **OTE range**: $180,000–$320,000+ (varies by company, segment, region, quota attainment)

For example, a mid-market SE at Salesforce might have $150K base + $100K variable = $250K OTE. Enterprise SEs at AWS or Databricks push toward $350K+ in OTE. However, the variable piece requires quota—miss it consistently, and compensation drops.

**Source data**: OnTarget Earnings (OTE) is the industry standard. Check [levels.fyi Sales Roles](https://www.levels.fyi) and [Blind sales compensation threads](https://www.teamblind.com) for real-time data.

### Core Skills
- **Technical credibility**: Deep product knowledge AND general cloud/architecture chops. You must understand the customer's stack, not just your own.
- **Demo delivery**: Live demos under pressure. This requires practice and composure.
- **Listening and diagnosis**: Understanding customer pain *before* pitching. A good SE asks questions more than talks.
- **Objection handling**: "Our system is too tightly integrated" → "Let me show you the integration APIs." Requires empathy + technical backbone.
- **RFP response**: Often SEs write technical sections of RFP responses. This is doc-heavy work.
- **Sales psychology**: Understanding deal stages, leverage points, and stakeholder motivations.

### Books
- **"Mastering Technical Sales"** by Jeff Cohen — The canonical SE playbook. Covers discovery, positioning, objection handling, and territory expansion.
- **"The Trusted Advisor Fieldbook"** by Charles H. Green, David Maister — Sales is trust. How to build it. Less SE-specific, but essential mindset.
- **"Cracking the Sales Management Code"** by Jason Jordan, Michelle Vazzana — If you're climbing toward sales management, this dissects the org structure you'll manage.

### YouTube Channels & Communities
- **[PreSales Collective](https://www.presalescollective.com)** — The main SE community. Training, events, job board. This is *the* SE hub.
- **[Solution Engineering Podcast](https://www.presalescollective.com/podcast)** — Hosted by PreSales Collective. Real SE war stories.
- **Salesforce Trailblazer SE Community** — Large, vendor-specific SE network.
- **[Sales Hacker](https://www.saleshacker.com)** — Broader sales content, but good for SEs in first 2 years.

### Why It's Different
Unlike pure IC engineering, SE compensation is tied to team revenue. You celebrate wins with commissions, but you also feel market downturns. The role demands speaking, travel, and customer intimacy. For people who love problem-solving *and* people, it's a natural fit. For people who recharge alone, it's exhausting.

## 2. Developer Relations / Developer Advocate

### The Role
A Developer Advocate (DA) is the vendor's ambassador to the developer community. You write blogs, speak at conferences, create YouTube tutorials, contribute to open-source projects, and listen to developer feedback to shape product roadmap.

This is NOT developer marketing. A true DA writes real code (not just demo-ware), maintains credibility in the community, and reports truth back to product even when it's uncomfortable.

### Career Arc
- **Senior Engineer** → **Developer Advocate** → **Senior Developer Advocate / DA Manager** → **Head of DevRel** → **VP Community / VP of Developer Experience**

Alternatively, some DAs move sideways into Product Management, Marketing, or return to IC engineering with stronger communication skills.

### Specialty Splits
DevRel orgs split advocates by domain:

1. **Content DA**: Writing, blogging, video tutorials. Heavy on async communication.
2. **Community DA**: Running Slack/Discord, moderation, events, Slack AMA. Heavy on community management.
3. **Field DA**: Conference speaking, traveling, in-person workshops. High visibility, high travel.
4. **Technical Marketing DA**: Bridge between DevRel and marketing. Content that converts.

Most individual contributors span two of these, but specialists exist at larger companies.

### Core Skills
- **Writing**: Blog posts, tutorials, API documentation. Your words represent the brand.
- **Speaking**: Conference talks, webinars, live coding. Ability to hold an audience.
- **Real code**: Not "pseudocode on slides." Ship open-source libraries, maintain repos, contribute to community projects. Your code credibility is everything.
- **Empathy**: Listening to developer frustration without defensiveness. Understanding why a feature is broken *for users*.
- **Product thinking**: How do devs actually use this? What's the hidden friction? Report back to product.

### Books
- **"Developer Relations: How to Build and Grow a Successful Developer Program"** by Caroline Lewko and James Parton — The modern playbook. Covers hiring, metrics, strategy, and avoiding common pitfalls.
- **"The Business Value of Developer Relations"** by Mary Thengvall — Quantifying ROI of DevRel (which is hard but important).
- **"Developers as Learners"** by Cassidy Williams — Less about role, more about how devs think and learn. Shapes content.

### Salary Data
- **Entry (DA)**: $130,000–$160,000
- **Senior DA**: $160,000–$220,000
- **Head of DevRel**: $200,000–$350,000+ (plus equity in startups)

**Source data**: [levels.fyi DevRel roles](https://www.levels.fyi), [Blind DevRel threads](https://www.teamblind.com), company career pages.

### Communities & Events
- **[DevRel Collective](https://www.devrel.net)** — Slack workspace, ~4000 practitioners. The main hub.
- **[DevRel.net](https://www.devrel.net)** — Content hub, job board, member directory.
- **[DevRelCon](https://devrelcon.net)** — Annual conference (multiple regions). Networking + talks.
- **[Write the Docs](https://www.writethedocs.org)** — Overlaps with DevRel, especially on docs-focused advocates.

### YouTube
- **[Foundations of Developer Relations](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLjqohSCwdEZVnQHd7r2HLi6EJgP2R_4b5)** — Official DevRel training series.
- Conference channels: **KubeCon North America** (CNCF), **FOSDEM**, **JSConf**, **PyCon**.

### Why It's Different
DevRel is the anti-silo role. You operate across product, marketing, engineering, and community. Compensation is lower than pure IC engineering at equivalent levels, but the role has high visibility and influence on product direction. Travel and speaking can be a perk or a burden depending on personality.

## 3. Technical Program Manager (TPM)

### The Role
A TPM coordinates cross-team technical initiatives too complex for a single team to own. Think "we're migrating our infrastructure to Kubernetes across 20 services and 8 teams, and the project spans 18 months." TPMs map dependencies, unblock teams, communicate status to executives, and keep timelines realistic.

**Key difference from PM**: PMs define *what* to build. TPMs coordinate *how* complex technical work gets done.

**Key difference from EM**: EM is responsible for engineer growth and hiring. TPM is responsible for the program finishing on time.

### Career Arc
- **Senior Engineer or Senior PM** → **TPM** → **Senior TPM** → **Principal TPM** → **Director of TPM / VP Engineering Programs**

Some TPMs move to product management, others to executive roles. The program management foundation is valuable everywhere.

### Core Skills
- **Technical depth**: You don't need to be an expert in every stack, but you need to *understand* the systems involved. A TPM managing a microservices migration must grasp service boundaries, data consistency, and deployment strategies.
- **Program management**: Critical path analysis, dependency mapping, risk register, stakeholder comms.
- **Ambiguity tolerance**: TPM projects have vague requirements. You clarify them before they explode.
- **Executive comms**: Translating technical complexities into business impact for leadership.
- **Conflict resolution**: When team A's deadline conflicts with team B's resource constraint, you referee.

### Reality Check: Where TPM Is Actually Real
TPM is a viable career path *only* at:
- **FAANG** (Amazon, Meta, Google, Apple, Netflix)
- **Hyperscalers** (Microsoft Azure, AWS, DigitalOcean)
- **Large enterprise** (Stripe, Databricks, Figma, etc.)

At small startups, the CTO or VP Engineering does this work—there's no dedicated role.

### Compensation
- **TPM (L4/L5 at Meta, L5 at Amazon)**: $180,000–$250,000 base + significant equity
- **Senior TPM (L5-L6)**: $220,000–$320,000 base + equity
- **Principal TPM**: $280,000–$400,000+ depending on company

**Source data**: [levels.fyi TPM roles](https://www.levels.fyi) — Check Amazon, Meta, Google tabs for real bands.

### Books
- **"The Manager's Path"** by Camille Fournier — Chapter on TPM. Essential read; discusses the transition from IC to program leadership.
- **"Project to Product"** by Mik Kersten — Reframing technical work as flow through the org. Influenced Google and others on TPM mindset.
- **"An Elegant Puzzle: Systems of Engineering Management"** by Will Larson — Written by a Stripe EM. Discusses program structure, team scaling, and strategic technical work.
- **"Accelerate"** by Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, Gene Kim — Data on what engineering practices actually improve delivery speed. The book TPMs cite.

### Communities
- **[Reforge](https://www.reforge.com)** — High-end continuing education. "Technical Program Management" course is well-regarded (not free, ~$500–$1000).
- **Internal company TPM guilds**: Most large companies have Slack channels or communities of practice.
- **LinkedIn groups**: "Technical Program Managers" is active.

### Why It's Different
TPM is appealing to engineers who like systems thinking but don't want to write code full-time. It's intellectually stimulating (complex coordination problems) and visible (you interface with C-level). However, the role exists only at scale. If you stay at small companies, this career path evaporates.

## 4. Technical Writer / Documentation Engineer

### The Role
Technical Writers create documentation: API docs, user guides, tutorials, architecture diagrams, and learning content. Increasingly, TW roles evolve into *Documentation Engineer*, meaning you own the tooling, automation, and infrastructure around docs (CI/CD for docs, versioning, search, analytics).

Documentation is often undervalued. A great TW saves support tickets, reduces onboarding friction, and unblocks customers. Paradoxically, it's less prestigious in tech than it deserves to be.

### Career Arc
- **Junior Technical Writer** → **Technical Writer** → **Senior Technical Writer** → **Lead Technical Writer / Documentation Engineering Manager** → **Director of Documentation / Head of DocEng**

Alternative: TW → Product Manager (your docs work makes you understand user needs).

### Specialty Splits
- **API Documentation**: OpenAPI specs, SDK docs, endpoint references. Heavy technical depth.
- **End-User Documentation**: How-to guides, troubleshooting, FAQs. Heavy on clarity and empathy.
- **Learning Content**: Courses, tutorials, workshops. Heavy on pedagogy.
- **Internal / Process Documentation**: Platform runbooks, architecture decision records, policy docs. Often invisible but critical.

### Core Skills
- **Writing fundamentals**: Clear, concise, audience-appropriate prose. No jargon unless explained.
- **Information architecture**: How to organize complex information so users *find* what they need.
- **Markdown / reStructuredText**: Industry-standard doc formats.
- **Static site generators**: Hugo, MkDocs, Docusaurus, Sphinx. Docs *as code* is the modern standard.
- **OpenAPI / API spec tooling**: Swagger, AsyncAPI. Knowing how to work with API specs.
- **Git and version control**: Docs live in repos. You PR, review, CI/CD test doc builds.
- **Basic HTML/CSS**: You'll customize templates, debug styling issues.

### Certifications & Communities
- **Society for Technical Communication (STC)**: Professional org. Certificates in "Technical Writing" and "Information Design." Not required, but credible.
- **[Write the Docs](https://www.writethedocs.org)**: THE community for technical writers.
  - Main site: writethedocs.org
  - [Write the Docs Slack](https://www.writethedocs.org/slack/) — ~10,000 members. Active help channel.
  - [Write the Docs Conference](https://www.writethedocs.org/conf/): Annual events in Portland (North America) and Prague (Europe).
  - [WTD Podcast](https://www.writethedocs.org/podcast/) — Bi-weekly episodes on docs topics.

### Books
- **"Docs for Developers"** by Bhatti, Breech, Gingel — Modern, practical. API docs, SDKs, example code. The 2020s TW bible.
- **"Every Page Is Page One"** by Mark Baker — Revolutionary approach to docs structure. Treat every page as a possible entry point, not a linear manual.
- **"The Product Is Docs"** by Splunk team — Why good documentation is a product feature.
- **"Modern Technical Writing"** by Andrew Etter — Short, online-first. Covers Markdown, static site generators, and docs mindset.

### Salary Data
- **TW (entry)**: $80,000–$110,000
- **Senior TW**: $110,000–$160,000
- **Lead TW / Doc Eng Manager**: $140,000–$200,000+

**Source data**: 
- [Bureau of Labor Statistics 27-3042 (Technical Writers)](https://www.bls.gov/oes/current/oes273042.htm) — Government baseline.
- [levels.fyi Tech Writer roles](https://www.levels.fyi)
- [Glassdoor Technical Writer salary](https://www.glassdoor.com/Salaries/technical-writer-salary-SRCH_KO0,17.htm)

Note: TW salaries are historically lower than equivalent-level IC engineers. This is slowly changing as companies recognize doc infrastructure value.

### YouTube
- **[Write the Docs conference talks](https://www.youtube.com/c/WritetheDocs)** — Real problems, real solutions.
- **[Daniele Procida - Diátaxis](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t4vKPhjcMZo)** — How to structure documentation. Influential framework.

### Why It's Different
TW is appealing to people who love clarity, systems, and helping others learn. It's less "problem-solving" and more "communication." Salary is lower than IC engineering, but so is the stress. Many TWs love the field because it values quality over speed.

## 5. Technical Recruiter / Sourcer

### The Role
Technical recruiters hire engineers. They source candidates, screen technical fit, manage pipelines, and close offers. The role splits into:

1. **In-house**: Work for a company, hire for their engineering org.
2. **Agency / Staffing**: Work for a recruitment firm, place candidates at multiple companies. Higher commission, less job security.

### Career Arc
- **Recruiter (generalist or technical)** → **Senior Recruiter / Tech Sourcer** → **Recruiting Manager** → **Director of Recruiting** → **VP of People / Head of Talent**

### Core Skills
- **Technical fundamentals**: You don't need to code, but you must understand tech stack basics. "What does a DevOps engineer actually do?" If you can't answer, you'll waste candidates' and hiring managers' time.
- **Sourcing**: LinkedIn, GitHub, community groups. Finding candidates before they post on job boards.
- **Screening**: Phone screens are rapid-fire. Can you assess technical culture fit in 15 minutes?
- **Relationship building**: Candidates talk. Treat people well, and they refer friends.
- **Closing**: Offer negotiation, handling counteroffers, selling your company.

### Certifications
- **LinkedIn Recruiter Certification**: Free, online. Not deep, but credible signal.
- **Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM)**: General HR certs. Less technical.

### Books
- **"Who: The A Method for Hiring"** by Geoff Smart and Randy Street — How to hire A players. Industry standard.
- **"Work Rules!"** by Laszlo Bock (Google's former People Ops head) — Hiring philosophy and ops. Influential.
- **"Lean In"** by Sheryl Sandberg — Relevant sections on hiring and career navigation.

### Salary Data
- **Recruiter (in-house)**: $70,000–$110,000 base + variable (10–30% of salary).
- **Senior Recruiter / Sourcer**: $100,000–$160,000 base + 20–40% variable.
- **Recruiting Manager**: $120,000–$200,000 depending on company size.

Agency roles pay more commission but less base.

**Source data**: [levels.fyi Recruiting roles](https://www.levels.fyi), Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary.

### Communities
- **[LinkedIn Recruiter Community](https://www.linkedin.com/groups/3719622/)** — Active group.
- **Internal company recruiting Slack channels**.

### Why It's Different
Tech recruiting is underrated. Great recruiters make or break a company's growth. The role is relationship-heavy and deadline-driven. If you like mentoring people into new careers and closing deals, it's satisfying. If you hate small talk or lose patience with indecision, it's torture.

## 6. IT Trainer / Instructor

### The Role
IT Trainers teach technology. This could be in-person boot camps, vendor-authorized training (Microsoft, AWS, Cisco), corporate training, or online courses.

### Types
1. **Vendor-Authorized Trainer**: Teach official certifications (Microsoft Certified Trainer, AWS Authorized Instructor, Cisco CCSI).
2. **Boot Camp Instructor**: Full-time at coding schools or training orgs.
3. **Corporate Trainer**: Internal training for company employees.
4. **Online Course Creator**: Udemy, Pluralsight, A Cloud Guru. Passive income potential.

### Certifications
- **Microsoft Certified Trainer (MCT)**: Teach Microsoft official courses. Requires current Microsoft cert + pass background check.
- **AWS Authorized Instructor (AAI)**: AWS equivalent. Teach AWS courses.
- **Cisco CCSI**: Cisco Certified Systems Instructor. Teach Cisco routing, switching, security.

Each vendor has requirements (current certs, passing exams, teaching materials).

### Compensation
Highly variable:
- **Boot camp instructor**: $60,000–$100,000 full-time. Some profit-sharing models.
- **Vendor-authorized trainer (contract)**: $50–$150/hour depending on course, location, demand.
- **Online course creator (Udemy)**: Highly variable. Popular instructors earn $10,000–$100,000/year per course (total, not per student).
- **Corporate trainer**: $80,000–$130,000depending on industry.

**Reality**: Most trainers combine multiple income streams (in-person + online + corporate contracts).

### Why It's Different
Trainer roles appeal to people who want to teach but aren't interested in academia. Autonomy is high (you design curriculum), but income can be unpredictable if contract-based. Online course income is mostly passive once created, but audience-building is hard.

## 7. Open Source Maintainer (Sponsored)

### The Role
Some developers make a living maintaining open-source libraries and frameworks. GitHub Sponsors, OpenCollective, and Tidelift allow communities (and companies) to fund maintainers.

**Reality check**: This is not a reliably full-time living for most. It works best if:
- Your project is widely used (Rails, Vue, NumPy, etc.).
- You have corporate sponsors (major companies like Google or Meta funding maintainers).
- You pair it with consulting or employment.

### Funding Models
1. **GitHub Sponsors**: Users or companies sponsor you directly. Typical: $50–$500/month per sponsor.
2. **OpenCollective**: Crowdfunded collective. Transparent finances. Typical: $500–$5,000/month for mid-size projects.
3. **Tidelift**: Corporate-backed. Companies pay maintainers to ensure license compliance and security patches. Ranges widely.
4. **Corporate sponsorship**: Google funds Kubernetes maintainers. Mozilla funds Rust ecosystem projects.

### Real Numbers
According to surveys:
- **Median**: $1,000–$3,000/month for active maintainers of moderately popular projects.
- **Top tier** (Vue, Rails creators): $50,000+/month.
- **Median reality**: Most full-time maintainers are actually employed by a company (Google, Netflix, Meta) *and* maintain OSS part-time.

### Resources
- **[GitHub Sponsors](https://github.com/sponsors)** — Direct sponsorships.
- **[OpenCollective](https://opencollective.com)** — Transparent fundraising.
- **[Tidelift](https://tidelift.com)** — Commercial funding.
- **[Linux Foundation Career Programs](https://www.linuxfoundation.org)** — Training and job board for OSS practitioners.

### Books
- **"Working in Public"** by Nadia Eghbal — How maintainers actually work, sustain, and burn out. Essential.

### Why It's Different
OSS is a calling for many. The autonomy is unmatched. But income is unstable. Most people who do this successfully:
1. Work at a company that sponsors them (Google, Meta, Microsoft).
2. Consult on the side.
3. Teach courses on their library.
4. Have independent wealth or a partner's income.

## 8. Independent Consultant / Contractor

### The Role
You sell expertise directly. Short-term projects, specific problems, or ongoing retainers. You can work as:
- **Sole proprietor**: W9 (1099), self-employed.
- **S-Corp or LLC**: Contract with your own company.
- **Agency consultant**: Work for a consulting firm.

### Finding Work
1. **Toptal**: Vetted freelance platform. High bar, higher rates. ($60–$150+/hour).
2. **Gun.io**: Specialized contractor board for software engineers.
3. **Arc.dev**: Y Combinator-backed. Vetted contractor network.
4. **Upwork**: Broad, lower-bar. Rates vary wildly.
5. **Direct**: Networking, referrals. Best margins.

### Rate Setting
- **Day rate**: $1,500–$5,000/day depending on experience and market.
- **Hourly**: $75–$200+/hour (higher in FAANG exit communities).
- **Retainer**: $5,000–$50,000+/month for ongoing work.

**Book**: **"Million-Dollar Consulting"** by David Weiss — The canonical guide. How to position, sell, and scale consulting. Essential reading.

### Legal Structure
- **Sole proprietor (W9)**: Simplest, but you're personally liable. No separation.
- **S-Corp or LLC**: Separate entity, liability shield, tax benefits. More complexity, more costs. If you're making $100k+/year consulting, an S-Corp usually saves money.

Consult an accountant in your state. This varies by location.

### Compensation
Real numbers:
- **Early career contractor**: $50–$80/hour.
- **Mid-career**: $100–$150/hour.
- **Senior / niche**: $150–$300+/hour.
- **Day rates**: $1,500–$5,000+/day.

Retainers and equity deals can be much higher, but less predictable.

### Why It's Different
Consulting is autonomy with income volatility. You control your time but hunt for clients. Some people thrive; others hate the feast-famine cycle. Most successful consultants either:
1. Stay plugged into their network (constant referral flow).
2. Build a personal brand (Twitter, blog, speaking).
3. Specialize ruthlessly (vs. generalist, which commoditizes).

## 9. Founder / Startup CTO

### The Role
You start a company and run its technical vision and early product. As CTO you set architecture, hire engineers, and build the initial product. Your title is "Founder" or "CTO" (often both in year one).

### Reality Check
- **Success rate**: ~10% of startups succeed (exit, profitability, or strong growth). Most fail.
- **Time investment**: 60–80-hour weeks for years. Burnout is real.
- **Compensation**: Low (if any) salary, but equity that *could* become significant.

### Resources
- **[Y Combinator Startup School](https://www.startupschool.org)** — Free online curriculum. Covers ideation, product, fundraising.
- **[Y Combinator Founder Resources](https://www.ycombinator.com/resources)** — Curated guides, talks, mentorship.
- **HackerNews**: Visit https://news.ycombinator.com — Founder community, open discussions on fundraising and strategy.

### Books
- **"The Hard Thing About Hard Things"** by Ben Horowitz — Raw, honest take on building companies. What happens when everything breaks.
- **"Zero to One"** by Peter Thiel — Philosophy of building unique products. Contrarian thinking.
- **"The Lean Startup"** by Eric Ries — Build fast, learn from users, iterate. Less romantic, more pragmatic than Thiel.
- **"Crossing the Chasm"** by Geoffrey Moore — How to move from early adopters to mainstream market. Not obvious and often ignored.

### Salary Data
- **Founder salary (pre-seed/seed)**: Often $0–$60,000 depending on runway and founder agreement.
- **Equity**: Typically 20–40% per co-founder depending on vesting and funding dilution.
- **Upside**: If exit happens, equity compounds. Many CTOs own 10–20% at exit and land $1M–$10M+. But most exits are acquihires ($0–$500K per founder) or failures.

### Why It's Different
Founder is not a career lane; it's a life bet. You're trading salary security and sanity for potential outsized upside and autonomy. Statistically, you'll fail and restart. But if you succeed, the control and impact are unmatched.

## Why These Paths Matter

The tech industry often measures success by the IC ladder: junior, senior, staff, principal. But that's one narrow path. These nine lanes offer:

- **Different compensation models**: SE + variable; TW is more stable but lower; Founder is all upside.
- **Different skill priorities**: Sales Engineer needs people skills. TPM needs systems thinking. DevRel needs writing.
- **Different lifestyles**: Consultant has autonomy and travel. DevRel might mean 100 flights/year. Internal TPM is stable.
- **Different advancement ceilings**: TW maxes out around director. SE can push to VP Sales. DevRel can reach VP Community.

The key question: **What are you optimizing for?** If it's money and prestige, the IC ladder at a big tech company is hard to beat. If it's autonomy, the consultant or maintainer route is better. If it's impact on community, DevRel wins. If it's growth, founder is the accelerant.

None of these paths are "backup options." They're intentional trades.
